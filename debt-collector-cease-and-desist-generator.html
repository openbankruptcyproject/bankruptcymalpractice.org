How to use this letter

Send by certified mail. Use U.S. Postal Service certified mail with return receipt requested. Keep your copy of the letter, the certified-mail receipt, and the green return-receipt card (or its electronic equivalent) together as a single packet. The postmark and tracking number are your proof of delivery.

What the law says happens after this letter. Once a debt collector receives a written notice from you that you refuse to pay the debt or that you wish them to cease further communication, the FDCPA prohibits all further communication with you about the debt — except (a) one notice that they will cease, or (b) one notice of a specific legal remedy they intend to invoke.

Continued violation triggers a private right of action. Each subsequent communication after receipt of this letter is a separate FDCPA violation. The statute provides for actual damages, statutory damages of up to $1,000 per case, and reasonable attorney fees and costs. Document every additional contact with date, time, method, and content.

Important limit. The FDCPA covers third-party debt collectors, not (in most cases) the original creditor collecting in its own name. If your contact is from the original creditor, this letter may not apply — though state debt-collection statutes and the FCRA may still protect you.
